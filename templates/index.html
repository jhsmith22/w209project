Definitions

  • Firearm Death: The CDC includes the following as part of firearm deaths: Homicide, Suicide, Unintentional, Legal intervention/operations of war, and Undetermined injury from a firearm.
  • Injury Death: The CDC includes the following as part of injury deaths: Cut/Piercing, Drowning, Fire/Flame, Firearm, Other land transport, Poisoning, Suffocation, or Other or Unspecified.
  • Mass Shooting: The Violence Project uses the following definition for a mass shooting: “…a multiple homicide incident in which four or more victims are murdered with firearms—not including the offender(s)—within one event, and at least some of the murders occurred in a public location or locations in close geographical proximity (e.g., a workplace, school, restaurant, or other public settings), and the murders are not attributable to any other underlying criminal activity or commonplace circumstance (armed robbery, criminal competition, insurance fraud, argument, or romantic triangle).”
  • Deaths Per 100,000 Capita: We present death rates as per 100,000 capita when comparing data across time or across states so there is an apples-to-apples comparison for death rates. This is necessary because population size varies across time and across states. This means we divide by the respective population size in a given year or a given state, as relevant.
  • Not Hispanic or Latino: Refers to Americans who are classified by the United States Census as "white" and are not of Hispanic heritage (Bureau, U.S. Census. "U.S. Census website". United States Census Bureau. Retrieved 19 August 2017.)
  • Cook's Distance: Statistical method used to detect outliers in Ordinary Least Squares regression. The method is further described in this documentation: https://www.scikit-yb.org/en/latest/api/regressor/influence.html
  • Restrictive and Permissive Gun Laws: The RAND gun law database classifies whether each state's gun law had a permissive or restrictive effect relative to the legal regime prior to the implementation. If the new law imposes greater restrictions on gun access or use, they categorize it as having a restrictive effect. If it eases access or use restrictions, they categorize it as having a permissive effect"
  • Gun Policy Permissiveness Score We aggregate the gun laws for each state into an aggregate "Gun Policy Permissiveness Score" using the law classifications from the RAND gun law database. RAND assesses each law as either "permissive" or "restrictive." We assign each permissive law with 2 points, each restrictive law with -2 points, and assign 1 point if no law exists in that category. RAND breaks out each law class for its application to handguns and long guns, and we assign points accordingly. For example, if a state had restrictive background check laws for handguns and for long guns, that totals -4 points (-2 for handguns and -2 for long guns). We also apply our own weights from 1 to 5 to the different law classes, to represent the estimated relative importance of each law class in affecting gun use. We assign registration a 5, as the most impactful law class. We assign carrying a concealed weapon (ccw), Castle Doctrine, and permit to purchase a 3. We assign firearm sales restrictions a 2. We assign background checks, open carry, safety training, and local laws preempted a 1, as the least impactful law classes.

Key Data Limitations

  • We do not have data on firearm injuries. Firearm injuries are an important part of understanding the full impact of gun violence in the United States. We will continue to seek out reliable sources of gun injuries for future releases.
